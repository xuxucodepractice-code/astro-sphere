Research is intellectual work, but it is also endurance work.

A PhD is not only a test of whether someone can understand difficult ideas. It is also a test of whether they can return to a problem again and again, organize uncertainty, recover from failed attempts, and keep improving without needing immediate reward.

That is why I care about the systems around my work, not just the work itself.

## Digital Systems as Thinking Infrastructure

I have spent years building personal productivity systems with tools such as Obsidian and Notion. I do not see these tools as aesthetic dashboards. I see them as thinking infrastructure.

My system is designed around a simple problem: important ideas rarely arrive fully formed. They begin as fragments, project notes, paper summaries, experiment observations, questions, and reflections. If those fragments are not captured and revisited, they disappear before they can become useful.

For research, this matters. A good note system helps me:

- separate observations from conclusions;
- track why an experiment failed;
- connect concepts across finance, AI, and robotics;
- preserve questions that may become future research ideas;
- review long-term goals without losing sight of weekly execution.

The point is not to become more busy. The point is to become more coherent.

## Sport and Research Discipline

I also train consistently. Tennis twice a week and Hyrox-style conditioning have become part of how I maintain energy, patience, and resilience.

Sport gives feedback that is difficult to avoid. If my movement is late, the ball is gone. If my conditioning is weak, the workout exposes it. If I become emotional, my execution becomes worse.

That feedback loop is useful for research temperament. It teaches me to respect fundamentals, stay calm under pressure, and improve through repeated small corrections.

Research has a similar rhythm. A model fails. A proof does not close. A result looks promising and then collapses under a better validation split. The useful response is not panic. It is diagnosis.

## History and Philosophy

Outside technical work, I am drawn to Chinese history and philosophy. Part of the attraction is scale. Historical thinking makes individual problems feel less absolute. It reminds me that systems, incentives, institutions, and human behavior interact over long periods.

That perspective matters for AI and finance. Models do not exist in empty space. They operate inside social, economic, and institutional environments. A technically impressive system can still fail if it ignores incentives, feedback loops, or human behavior.

Philosophy also helps me think about ambition. I want to work seriously, but not frantically. I want to build competence over years, not only chase short bursts of performance.

## Why This Belongs on an Academic Website

For a PhD application, personal traits should not be decoration. They should explain why a person is prepared for long research work.

My systems, sport, and humanities interests all point toward the same underlying trait: I care about sustained development.

I am learning to become someone who can hold a difficult question for a long time, keep records honestly, train consistently, and return to the work with more clarity each time.

That, to me, is part of becoming a researcher.
